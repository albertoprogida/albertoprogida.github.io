EuRoC 2022: Darwin III Post-Flight Report

October 17, 2022

On Saturday, October 15th, 2022, the Darwin III launch vehicle took to the skies above the Santa Margarida Army Base in Portugal. Conceptualized and manufactured by the Endeavour student group at the University of Edinburgh, this mission aimed for a target apogee of 9,000 meters. This report details the technical achievements, flight analysis, and lessons learned from the European Rocketry Challenge (EuRoC).

The Launch Vehicle: Darwin III

Darwin III is a high-performance sounding rocket powered by a Cesaroni Technologies Inc. (CTI) O3400 solid rocket motor. Weighing in at 34.334kg, the vehicle was constructed using advanced Carbon Fiber Reinforced Polymer (CFRP) composites and high-strength 7075-T6 Aluminium alloy. This structure was designed to withstand the immense 500kg thrust load and accelerations reaching 150 m/sÂ².

The design builds upon the team's previous success with Darwin II, incorporating a modular avionics bay, an SRAD (Student Research and Developed) active airbrake system for precise apogee targeting, and a deployable payload system.

Payload & Scientific Mission

The mission carried two scientifically significant experiments, recognized by the European Space Agency Payload Award as the best among over 20 competitors:

  • Muon Detector: A ruggedized version of MIT's CosmicWatch, designed to collect muon count data and explore relativistic effects by comparing flight rates to sea-level baselines.
  • Fluid Dynamics Experiment: A wide-angle lens camera system observing the behavior of propylene glycol in micro-gravity to validate CFD simulations.

Flight Analysis

Ignition occurred successfully at 12:00:28 GMT following a switch to a more energetic igniter. The vehicle performed a nominal 6.2-second burn, accelerating rapidly through the dense lower atmosphere. A sonic boom was reported by Mission Control, consistent with trajectory simulations predicting supersonic flight.

"The vehicle ascended remarkably straight - a testament to the stiffness of the team's structure and the accuracy of the fin construction."

However, telemetry was lost prematurely due to high interference and velocity. While GPS signals were temporarily regained after the supersonic phase, confirming structural integrity through Max-Q, the vehicle failed to deploy its nosecone at apogee. This resulted in a ballistic re-entry.

Recovery & Lessons Learned

Darwin III impacted the ground at approximately 251 m/s, penetrating 600mm into the soil. While 80% of the vehicle disintegrated upon impact, the recovery of components allowed for a detailed failure analysis. The root cause was identified as a failure in the nosecone separation mechanism at apogee.

Despite the off-nominal recovery, the mission was deemed a partial success, achieving 4 out of 6 mission goals. Key achievements included:

  • Validation of advanced composite manufacturing techniques.
  • Successful operation of student-developed avionics under high-G loads.
  • Recognition of scientific rigour by ESA.
  • Massive improvements in organizational efficiency, achieving a sub-30 minute assembly time in the field.
