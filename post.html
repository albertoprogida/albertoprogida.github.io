Breakthrough

Published on June 15, 2024 by Alberto

Something's been shifting over the past couple of months. In my adolescence, I went through some tough family experiences that pushed me to use school and university as an escape. I became a hardworking student and let education fully consume me. For years, it gave me structure, goals, and an objective to laser-focus on. But since leaving education, I've begrudgingly had the mental space to zoom out and think beyond the next lecture pack or problem set and I've come to a realisation. I've realised that I love a mission. I always have - scholarships, internships, fellowships, degrees, awards - these were all missions. But looking back, those missions never involved much personal risk. They were always aligned with traditional milestones, achievements that looked impressive and were ultimately safe. I think that came from the people around me. After the trauma my family went through, I was mostly surrounded by risk-averse voices. The ones who passed away were bold risk takers, and their influence slowly faded in favour of more comforting voices. But I had been around them long enough as a kid - I carry their DNA - and I now realise their mindset is actually part of me. The caution I've carried wasn't innate; it was something I wrapped around myself like a safety blanket after they were gone. Until recently, my internal drive for purpose fit neatly within that blanket because it was easy to align both into getting good grades or winning scholarships. But in the real world, that balance is breaking down. I've been struggling with direction, with what kind of career or life I want to build. The more I try to force myself into the polished, predictable cursus honorum of an engineer, the more I doubt it's what I truly want. And I think it's only now that everything's starting to click and shift into gear. I don't want to spend the next 40 years collecting skills like trophies. I don't want a job - I want a cause. I want to be consumed by the challenge of building something that matters. Tending a little garden isn't enough. I want to be the reason something changes forever.

Alberto Progida
